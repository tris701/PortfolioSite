Overview

At the end of my Motion for Computer Animation class, the final project tasked me with creating a 2-3 minute animation in 3dsmax, using what I’ve learned since the introductory class. The animation was required to have multiple cameras, properly rigged characters, and a plot. The project was the most challenging animation task I had dealt with up until that point, as it was my first serious attempt at a 3D animated short. As an aspiring 3D animator and artist, this project was an important step for me and taught me many things about animating and my own creative process.

Step 1: Conceptualization and Storyboarding

I’ve always had an interest in action choreography and cinematography and the creativity that goes into creating a great fight scene. Deciding the type of animation I wanted to do wasn’t hard, but the most difficult part of this stage was figuring out how to blend the choreography, plot, staging, and characters into one concise and appealing animation. I thought of many varied and ambitious ideas, but it was important to keep it feasible and realistic within the given timeframe.

I decided on a one-on-one choreographed fight scene with a simple plot to set the stage for the animation. The plot involves an agent character walking into a room several stories up in a tall building. The room is full of scattered furniture, with an old TV sitting on a table at the end of the room. The TV emerges as a mirrored version of the agent, attacking him and prompting a fight sequence. The TV mirrors agent’s appearance with an inverted color scheme, and fights using primarily kicks, in contrast to the agent who uses primarily his fists. At the end, the agent dodges an attack from the ghost, causing the ghost to fall out of the window. The setting was designed to allow big moments in the scene to take place. Important moments in the scene included a character crashing through a table, and the ghost falling out of the large window, which were scene elements I introduced early in the scene to hint at their importance.

I made two storyboards and two animatics, further refining my ideas in the second iteration. My storyboards aimed to show the most important moments in the plot and in the choreography while loosely planning the movements that would happen in between them. Deciding the movements that happen between the important moments is best done spontaneously in my experience, as better choreographed moments can come up in my mind spontaneously during the process of getting from moment A to moment B. Being flexible and open to different approaches has led to the creation of many unexpected but valuable ideas from my experience.

Step 2: Modeling and Animation

Turning the countless hours of planning and conceptualization into a real animation all depended on my ability to execute it in 3dsmax. As much as I loved planning the animation, I didn’t have much experience creating a longer animation in 3D, as I had previously only experimented with short lab assignments. Despite this, I was confident and excited to make something much better than anything I had produced up until that point in time.

I used the box modeling method to create models from scratch which was my first serious attempt at 3D character model after only experimenting with characters out of basic primitives. Because the course was designed around motion/animation, the scene itself was made very simply and with relatively low detail compared to the models. I made several attempts at different rigs and used manually made rigs for some of the introductory scenes, but for most of the animation I decided to stick to using CAT rigs since I was the most familiar with them and decided they’d help me better achieve what I had originally envisioned. I ran into many issues with the character models and envelopes while animating, and I did my best to minimize them within the timeframe. It was also one of my first attempts at utilizing animation layers, which was a different approach to animation that I wasn’t used to.

I took a great amount of consideration regarding the use of cameras and how they moved to capture different angles. It wasn’t perfect, but I was satisfied with how the camera moved for the most important parts of the action. I avoided using cuts for the longer stretches of choreography, and tried to move the camera in a way that matched the flow of movement.

Much of my effort went into animation, and it was difficult learning how to maneuver and position the rigs at a level of complexity I wasn’t very familiar with yet. I had also planned to fine-tune the speed and precision of the character’s motion more I than I ended up doing in the final product. But considering the time limitation and level of experience I had at the time, I’m glad I was able to create a final product that reflected my original concept fairly closely.

Conclusion

This project was an important step for me take as a 3D animator and was a notable learning experience for me, even with many problems and aspects that I underdelivered on. Being my first serious attempt at 3D project, I gained a lot of understanding with rigging and key frame animation, but most importantly I gained valuable experience with the 3D animation workflow and the amount of time that was needed to make every frame stand out.

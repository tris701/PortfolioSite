Overview

Much of my interest in animation comes from video games. The creative direction behind many of my favorite games have become some of my biggest sources of inspiration. These Pivot animations are some of my earliest attempts at animating character concepts that utilize important animation principles, such as anticipation, slow in and slow out, follow-through, etc.

Step 1: Conceptualization and Inspiration

The biggest inspiration behind these concepts came from the Fighting Game genre, which puts a strong focus on distinct and appealing character designs, as much of the gameplay is designed around them. Because Pivot is a 2D animation software, it is naturally reminiscent of many iconic fighting games. They tend to feature casts with diverse designs in both appearance and gameplay, and decisions behind their design elements and animations all serve towards the goal of helping establish the character’s core identity and personality. When initially conceiving these characters, I wanted to show a diverse set of motions that were unique among each character.

Step 2: Concept Sketches and Animation

Many of the ideas behind these animations came from me alongside a group of friends who enjoyed conceptualizing game ideas for fun. I created sketches and drawings of them to mimic “concept art” for the characters, then animated my take on how the characters might animate in a real game.

In fighting games, a balance must be struck between distinct animations and visually appealing animations. Strong poses and silhouettes are used to convey to the players what the character is doing so they can act accordingly. The 2D nature of Pivot lent itself well to the fighting game concept, and I did my best to make use of strong poses, slow in and slow out, follow through, and character appeal. The Pivot stick figures weren’t very complex rigs to animate with, but a little creativity was required to make certain moments pop, especially with such an old and limited software. The animations visualized the character concepts for the group and helped them continue to build off the concepts and add to the character.

Conclusion

I see these animations as an early exercise in showing character design through movement as well as creativity. While I want to specialize in 3D, working in 2D helped me develop a solid foundation for animating. My career goal is to be a part of making cool things for people to enjoy, and this was one of my first steps toward that dream.
